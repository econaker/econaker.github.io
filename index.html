The Natural Variability of Sea Floor Ecosystems

Sensitive environments such as the Arctic and deep ocean into which the energy industry is extending are generally poorly understood by the scientific community. Surveys are regularly discovering previously unknown habitats and marine wildlife. As a result, there is a lack of data which can be used as basis for historical understanding and future prediction of environmental conditions. However, it is apparent that all deep-sea environments support a wide range of flora and fauna that contribute significantly to global biodiversity and ecosystem function.

To date, only a limited number of deep-ocean sites have been monitored for periods exceeding five years. At these sites important annual cycles have been observed with considerable variability from year to year and changes in dominant fauna over decadal time scales. In the Pacific Ocean for example, there is evidence of a linkage between the surface climate and the deep sea environment. Major climatic events such as El Ni√±o Southern Oscillation - a band of anomalously warm ocean temperatures that occasionally develops of the western coast of South America - have influenced the deep sea floor community. Similar relationships between climate and deep-sea communities have been observed in the Atlantic Ocean.

As part of responsible exploration and production operations, it is vital the energy industry measure its impact on these sensitive areas. In order to do that, it is important to understand and distinguish the naturally occurring and spontaneous changes in sea floor fauna from any industry influences. Aker Solutions have developed the eCon observatory consisting of a suite of sensors that will monitor the sea-floor environment over the long-term and help the industry assess its impact. An eCon observatory has been deployed in the Ythan Estuary in the north-east of Scotland and the results are openly presented here.
