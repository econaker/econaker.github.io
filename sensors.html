Oxygen Sensor

Scientists have been routinely measuring oxygen concentrations in the ocean for more than a hundred years. With growing concerns about climate change, however, this parameter has increased in interest.

Oxygen distribution in the ocean depends on both biological processes, like the respiration of organisms, and on physical processes such as current flow. Changes in either of these processes should therefore lead to changes in oxygen distribution. As a result, dissolved oxygen can be viewed as a sensitive early warning system for global climate change in the ocean. Scientific studies show that this early warning system can detect the expected decrease in oxygen transport from the atmosphere into the ocean that is driven by global current and mixing processes, as well as possible changes in the marine biotic communities. In recent years, this knowledge has led to a resurgence of interest in the role oxygen plays in the field of global marine research.

The distribution of oxygen in the internal deep ocean is determined by a complex and not fully understood interplay of water circulation and bioÂ­logical productivity, which leads to oxygen consumption within the ocean. The highest oxygen concentrations are found at high latitudes, where the ocean is cold, well-mixed and ventilated. The mid-latitudes, by contrast, especially on the western coasts of the continents, are characterized by marked oxygen-deficient zones. Different groups of marine organisms react to the oxygen deficiency in different ways, For example, crustaceans and fish generally require higher oxygen concentrations than mussels or snails.

Fluorometer

The combination fluorometer and turbidity meter works by analysing data in two ways. The fluorometer detects phytopigments which can be related to the organic matter content (chlorophyll a) of the water column particle load identified by the turbidity meter. For animals living on or near the sea floor the seasonal input of food from the surface has a significant influence. The organic content of particles in the water column provides a measure of this energy input and is therefore an important consideration when studying sea floor impacts.

Doppler Flowmeter

The flow meter measures sea current direction and speed. It is an essential element in understanding the local sea floor environment and the influence flow has on the sea floor fauna.

Hydrophone

The passive acoustic sensor records the average background noise level. For an eCon observatory close to subsea infrastructure, with the combination of sensors and the camera it is possible to understand how changes in background noise affect animal behaviour.

Camera

The camera takes time-lapse photographs of the sea floor and associated fauna. In relatively unstudied areas, such as the Arctic and deep ocean, it is vital to obtain good quality high-resolution images of the indigenous fauna. The stills camera gives the flexibility required to correctly identify both invertebrates and fish. It is anticipated that these high-quality images will also have considerable education and public outreach potential.
